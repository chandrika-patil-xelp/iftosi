Diamond Polish

Polish in a diamond refers to the overall smoothness of individual facets of a diamond. A diamond with ideal polish is the glittering outcome of the superlative degree of skill involved in the polishing process. While Diamond Cut process impacts the polish of a diamond, polish maximizes its luster.

The polish grade of a diamond can be evaluated by applying the following parameters:

Polish defects on diamond surface
Excellent
The most preferable of the polish grade. Visibly glittering and superlative look. No imperfections are visible under 10x magnification.
Very Good
Noticeably brilliant with imperfections extremely tough to spot at 10x magnification.
Good
AImperfections are difficult to spot at 10x magnification.
Fair
Unimpressive luster and defects visible at 10x magnification. Efforts expose imperfections to the naked eye.
Poor
No luster with visible defects.

We at IFtoSI recommend buying diamonds with polish grade ranging between Excellent and Good, primarily because they are visibly appealing with no defects detectable even to the trained eyes. While customers should avoid buying the poor grade polish diamonds, stones belonging to the Fair grade sometimes make decent looks. We also advise buyers to avoid buying the Poor grade of diamonds, even if they are presented in a visually impressive manner.

Another significant aspect that determines diamonds’ qualitative and visual brilliance is their polish lines. Obviously, polish lines that require high degree of magnification renders a diamond’s polish rating excellent. Gemologists also consider certain other characteristics to evaluate a diamond’s grade. They include: Scratch, Lizard skin, Burn marks, Abrasion and Rough girdle.

Visible white lines on the surface of a diamond are referred to as Scratch, while Lizard skin points to uneven surface on a diamond. When a diamond is exposed to high temperatures the surface of it acquires a faint whitish layer referred to as Burn marks. A girdle left unpolished is a Rough girdle and a series of cut marks at junctions of facets are Abrasion.
