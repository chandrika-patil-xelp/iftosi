Diamond Cut

The cut on the diamond has the greatest influence on its outer aesthetics and hence, in most cases, ends up being primary determinant of the price. However, contrary to popular belief, cut does not merely mean the shape of the diamond. It is in essence a combination of multiple factors that determine the look of the diamond. They include the fineness and the polish, the consistency in the cut itself and proportions.

A diamond takes its shape from the brilliance of the cutter, who optimizes his skills to reduce waste and maximize the yield. Weight usually gains prominence over the appearance as buyers are often ready to shell out more money for a larger bauble. This factor renders cut grade important; it enables the buyer to choose stones in which carat weight has been retained, making the Cut fit into the category of Fair to Poor. However, it is important to note that a small, nicely cut diamond fetches a premium per-carat- price for the cutter.

The price of the smaller piece would be determined by the per-carat- price factor. The selling of a smaller diamond, therefore, does not necessarily mean low profit for merchant. Also, note that superior aesthetics and reduced output from the rough stone renders the smaller diamond piece expensive.

Diamond Cut Proportions

Proportion in a diamond cut comprises multiple factors and the inter-relationship among those factors. They include, among others, different angles of the diamond, its size and shape. Different combinations among these factors give rise to multiple possibilities in the diamond look and design. These possibilities determine how the diamond interacts with light.

A diamond proportioned well typically has each of its aspects appropriately positioned. Upon striking of light on a diamond, about one-fifth of it is reflected from the surface. Out of the remaining amount of light, a decent amount refracts through the diamond and the onlooker does not get to see any trace of it. Only the one-fifth of the light that reflects from the surface hits the observer’s eye. This light is considered as brilliance, scintillation and fire.

Light either bends while passing through the many facets of a diamond or the diamond makes light bounce back. This behavior of light – whether it refracts or reflects after hitting the diamond – depends on the angle of light that falls on the different facets of the diamond, making diamond cut an important aspect in the determination of a diamond’s quality and premiumness. Usually, there are three patterns in which light behaves depending on a diamond’s facet after hitting a diamond.

1.  

A diamond cut too shallow allows the light to hit the pavilion facet at a low angle and refracts. In this scenario, light escapes through the diamond’s bottom.

2.  

When a diamond cut is very deep, light hits the facet of the first pavilion at a sharp angle. It has enough spikes to reflect to the second pavilion; however, it hits the second pavilion at an angle very low. It makes the light pass through the facet, and then it makes its way through the diamond’s bottom.

3.  

When a diamond is cut well, light is reflected to the top of the stone. It occurs due to the angle in which light hits each pavilion facet.

Diamond Depth%

The measure of distance between the culet and the table is referred to as the Depth in a diamond cut. It is shown in millimeters, and is evaluated by dividing the depth of the diamond by the width of it.

Diamond Table %

When the width of the table facet is divided by the width of the diamond we get the table %.

grading diamond cut

A diamond’s Cut grade is the most pivotal aspect in assessing a diamond. The grade is determined by examining the collective effects of a diamond’s shapes and angles along with the impacts of girdle width, culet size, polish and symmetry on the exterior of a diamond.

Excellent, Very Good, and Good GIA Cut Grades

Usually, five scale parameters –Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, and Poor – are applied to determine a diamond’s cut grade. These can be elaborated as below:

Excellent
It exhibits optimal fire and brilliance. Presents the diamond in the most dazzling manner consequent to the penetration of all light into the diamond.
Very Good
Showcases itself as closely comparable to the Excellent Cut, but comes at a less premium price. Diamonds in this grade appropriately reflect almost all of the light and produces superior look under usual light scenarios.
Good
Cut grades falling under this parameter is often viewed as offering the best value for consumers’ money, due to its adequate pricing and above average exterior.
Fair
In the Fair Cut grade, light escapes a diamond either from the bottom or the sides, thereby reducing the surface brilliance.
Poor
A Poor Cut grade cannot hold any passing through it, thereby making it visibly unattractive to even common eyes.
